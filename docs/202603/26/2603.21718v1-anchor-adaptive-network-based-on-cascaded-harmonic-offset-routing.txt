Abstract 

Time series analysis plays a foundational role in a wide range of real-world applica-tions, yet accurately modeling complex non-stationary signals remains a shared challenge across downstream tasks. Existing methods attempt to extract features directly from one-dimensional sequences, making it difficult to handle the widely observed dynamic phase drift and discrete quantization error. To address this issue, we decouple temporal evolution into macroscopic physical periods and microscopic phase perturbations, and inject frequency-domain priors derived from the Real Fast Fourier Transform (RFFT) into the underlying spatial sampling process. Based on this idea, we propose a Frequency-Guided Deformable Module (FGDM) to adaptively compensate for microscopic phase deviations. Built upon FGDM, we further develop an Adaptive Network based on Cascaded Harmonic Offset Rout-ing (ANCHOR) as a general-purpose backbone for time-series modeling. Through orthogonal channel partitioning and a progressive residual architecture, ANCHOR efficiently decouples multi-scale harmonic features while substantially suppressing the computational redundancy of multi-branch networks. Extensive experiments demonstrate that ANCHOR achieves the best performance in most short-term forecasting sub-tasks and exhibits strong competitive-ness on several specific sub-tasks in anomaly detection and time-series classification, validat-ing its effectiveness as a universal time-series foundation backbone. 

# 1 Introduction 

Time series analysis has become the cornerstone of interdisciplinary research in modern data science, with its application scope encompassing dimensions ranging from proactive decision-making to system robustness evaluation [1, 2]. In climate modeling and power dispatching, high-precision time-series forecasting is a prerequisite for resource allocation optimization and disaster warning [3, 4]. In the fields of industrial Internet of Things (IIoT) and cybersecurity, real-time anomaly detection serves as a critical mechanism for identifying non-stationary de-viations, thereby ensuring the operational security of complex systems [5, 6]. Meanwhile, in biomedical signal processing, precise temporal classification is the core of achieving automated clinical diagnosis and pathological identification [7, 8]. Despite the divergence in task objec-tives, their essence relies on the profound deconstruction of latent physical laws within complex temporal signals. Real-world time-series data are often composed of intertwined multi-scale peri-odicities; these periodic features not only dictate predictive trends but also constitute structural fingerprints for classification and physical benchmarks for anomaly determination [3]. Con-sequently, constructing a universal backbone network capable of explicitly capturing physical periodic priors with cross-task generalizability has become a frontier challenge in the field [3, 9].

Prior to the deep learning era, models such as ARIMA (Auto-Regressive Integrated Moving Average) established linear modeling paradigms based on stationarity assumptions and autocor-relation structures through rigorous mathematical derivation [10]. To further capture dynamic evolution, the Holt-Winters exponential smoothing method introduced explicit modeling of trend and seasonal components, demonstrating superior interpretability in power load and commercial forecasting [11, 12]. With the advent of the Transformer, its robust architecture has demon-strated exceptional capabilities across various tasks, such as natural language processing [13, 14], speech recognition [15, 16], and computer vision [17, 18]. Similarly, Transformer architectures have focused on challenging domains within temporal tasks. For instance, in long-term forecast-ing, models like Informer [19], Autoformer [20], and PatchTST [21] utilize parallel computing to effectively span extreme temporal dimensions. Furthermore, in zero-shot forecasting, the capac-ity for large-scale pre-training on massive, heterogeneous multi-domain datasets—exemplified by TimeGPT-1 [22], Chronos [23], and Moirai [24]—has addressed many real-world cold-start prob-lems. Concurrently, CNN architectures have experienced a resurgence in temporal tasks due to their inherent local feature extraction and lower parameter costs. TimesNet demonstrated the advantages of convolution in processing periodic features [3]. Additionally, SCINet and MICN proved the efficacy of multi-scale convolution in modeling long-range dependencies [25, 26], while ModernTCN, through a modern pure convolutional architecture, has comprehensively surpassed mainstream Transformers in both accuracy and efficiency [27]. Despite the achievements of these models, each possesses distinct limitations. On one hand, although Transformers and their derived foundation models have achieved remarkable success in many fields, their efficacy in time-series forecasting is being scrutinized by the academic com-munity. Real-world temporal signals rely heavily on strict chronological order and continuous local dependencies, whereas the self-attention mechanism at the core of the Transformer exhibits inherent permutation invariance. As noted by Zeng et al. [28], even with the introduction of positional encodings, this attention mechanism inevitably leads to the loss of temporal infor-mation, causing its performance on many real datasets to fall below that of minimalist linear baselines. More recent theoretical research has further demonstrated, from the perspective of in-context learning, that models based on linear self-attention theoretically cannot achieve a lower expected Mean Squared Error than classical linear regression and are highly susceptible to pre-diction collapse during complex inference [29]. Crucially, due to its architecture, the Transformer remains stable only with large sample sizes and may overfit on small-sample datasets, thereby reducing computational efficiency [30]. These findings reveal a stark reality: blindly stacking attention mechanisms not only incurs expensive O(N 2) computational and memory overhead but also deviates from the physical essence of time-series data generation at the architectural level. On the other hand, although simple CNN architectures achieve performance comparable to Transformers on short-term datasets, their capacity is limited on long-term datasets such as ETTh1, indicating a deficiency in feature extraction for low-frequency, long-period data. Based on the aforementioned motivations, this paper proposes ANCHOR, an Adaptive Net-work based on Cascaded Harmonic Offset Routing. ANCHOR fundamentally abandons purely implicit spatial search routes and pioneeringly establishes frequency-domain harmonics as the “physical navigation” for time-domain geometric deformation. Specifically, we utilize the RFFT to extract explicit dominant physical periods of the signal, injecting them as prior anchors directly into the underlying dilation rate operators of multi-branch deformable convolutions. Furthermore, addressing the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) imbalance in multi-scale feature extrac-tion, this paper breaks the limitations of traditional static allocation by proposing an asymmetric routing mechanism: “small kernels for high-fidelity strong signals, large kernels for integral weak features.” This effectively balances the extraction weights of features across different frequency bands. Coupled with a progressive residual cascaded architecture, ANCHOR eliminates tem-poral entanglement, achieving a balance between strong signal sharpness and weak signal noise floors. Experimental results demonstrate that ANCHOR achieves superior performance across 2multiple tasks. The main contributions of this paper are as follows: 

• Revealing Prior-Guided Deformation Sparsity and Cross-Domain Phase Com-pensation Mechanisms. This paper is the first in time-series deep learning to establish frequency-domain harmonics as explicit navigators for spatial geometric deformation. By injecting RFFT-extracted dominant periods into the underlying dilation rate operators of de-formable convolutions and dynamically learning micro-deformations, the model cancels out the quantization errors inherent in the discrete picket-fence effect of the FFT in the time domain. 

• Creating a Universal Backbone Based on Energy-Compensated Frequency-Spatial Adaptation. Addressing the SNR imbalance of real-world non-stationary signals, this pa-per constructs a hierarchical progressive cascaded architecture based on channel partitioning and the asymmetric energy-compensation routing mechanism. This solves the phenomenon where strong signals are over-smoothed by large kernels and weak signals are submerged by noise in complex environments, achieving a profound decoupling of macroscopic patterns and microscopic perturbations at the source of feature flow. 

• Superior Performance Across Core Domains. The proposed model achieves best results in several core areas.

2.2 Frequency-Guided Deformable Module (FGDM) 

As previously discussed, while RFFT provides the model with global macroscopic harmonic priors, real-world physical signals evolve in a continuous time domain. The direct application of discrete Fourier transforms inevitably triggers the picket-fence effect. Consequently, the explicit physical period Tk extracted by the preceding module is a quantized discrete integer, possessing inherent rounding errors. Such discrete periods cannot align with absolute precision to the true continuous physical phase. 3Furthermore, traditional signal processing remedies have significant drawbacks: zero-padding leads to exponential expansion of dimensions and computational overhead without increasing information content, while flat-top windows widen the spectral main lobe and degrade frequency resolution. If Tk were used directly as a static dilation rate in conventional temporal convolu-tional networks, the phase misalignment would accumulate across spatial sampling layers. To circumvent these limitations, we propose a cross-domain error compensation architecture. This architecture utilizes the discrete period Tk merely as a coarse-grained spatial anchor and intro-duces 1D deformable convolution with continuous spatial interpolation to autonomously align and bridge the continuous phase in the time domain. As illustrated in Fig. 1, this cross-domain compensation mechanism treats the discrete period Tk as a coarse spatial anchor while leveraging continuous deformable sampling to recover the underlying continuous temporal phase. 

Figure 1: FGDM Structure To circumvent the limitations of traditional digital signal processing (DSP), specifically the high computational cost of zero-padding and the resolution degradation of flat-top windows, this paper proposes a novel cross-domain error compensation architecture. This architecture bypasses lossy repairs within the frequency domain, instead utilizing the quantized discrete pe-riod Tk solely as a coarse-grained spatial anchor. Furthermore, we introduce a one-dimensional deformable convolutional mechanism with continuous spatial interpolation capabilities. Diverg-ing from the original DCNv4, we first implement a 1D temporal adaptation of the DCNv4 [31] architecture. To accommodate 1D variable receptive field modeling, the convolutional front-end adopts an explicit execution mode, dynamically configuring padding and dilation during the forward pass. This ensures temporal length alignment and maintains consistency within the sampling control branches, thereby enabling the model to autonomously achieve alignment and closure of the continuous phase within the time domain. 2.3 The ANCHOR Model 

The FGDM can be integrated as a plug-and-play module into various models. We specifi-cally adopted the UniConvNet architecture, discovering that its inherent large-and-small kernel mechanism is perfectly suited for processing high-frequency and low-frequency signals respec-tively. Moreover, its native channel orthogonal partitioning strategy provides isolated physical subspaces for multi-scale frequency extraction. This enables FGDM to perform purely heteroge-neous processing on different periodic operators, suppressing cross-band information interference. Simultaneously, thanks to the channel partitioning strategy, this instantiated model signifi-cantly suppresses computational complexity and parameter redundancy compared to traditional multi-branch full-channel networks. 3.2 Anomaly Detection

Model evaluation is conducted across three widely used standard benchmarks: SMD [34] for service monitoring, SWaT [35] for water treatment scenarios, and PSM [36] for the aerospace domain. During the data preprocessing stage, this study adopts the sliding window mechanism from Anomaly Trans-former (2021), partitioning continuous sequences into non-overlapping independent segments. # 4 Conclusion 

This paper successfully constructs ANCHOR, a universal backbone network designed for multi-task time-series analysis. It innovatively integrates the macro-period priors derived from RFFT with the micro-phase tracking capabilities of FGDM. Furthermore, by leveraging an orthogo-nal subspace channel partitioning mechanism and a progressive residual fusion architecture, the model effectively decouples high-intensity signal sharpness from weak signal noise floors, while simultaneously mitigating computational redundancy within the multi-branch network. Exten-sive benchmarking and white-box representation analysis demonstrate that ANCHOR achieves superior cross-task generalizability and robust feature representation across three core tasks: long- and short-term forecasting, anomaly detection, and time-series classification.
