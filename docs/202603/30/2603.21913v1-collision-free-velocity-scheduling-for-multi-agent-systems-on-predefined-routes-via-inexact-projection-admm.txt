# Collision-Free Velocity Scheduling for Multi-Agent Systems on Predefined Routes via Inexact-Projection ADMM 

Seungyeop Lee 1 and Jong-Han Kim 1∗

Abstract — In structured multi-agent transportation systems, agents often must follow predefined routes, making spatial rerouting undesirable or impossible. This paper addresses route-constrained multi-agent coordination by optimizing waypoint passage times while preserving each agent’s assigned waypoint order and nominal route assignment. A differentiable surrogate trajectory model maps waypoint timings to smooth position profiles and captures first-order tracking lag, enabling pairwise safety to be encoded through distance-based penalties evaluated on a dense temporal grid spanning the mission horizon. The resulting nonlinear and nonconvex velocity-scheduling problem is solved using an inexact-projection Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) algorithm that combines structured timing updates with gradient-based collision-correction steps and avoids explicit integer sequencing variables. Numerical experiments on random-crossing, bottleneck, and graph-based network scenarios show that the proposed method computes feasible and time-efficient schedules across a range of congestion levels and yields shorter mission completion times than a representative hierarchical baseline in the tested bottleneck cases. 

I. INTRODUCTION Safe coordination is a fundamental requirement in multi-agent transportation systems such as urban air mobility (UAM), automated warehouse logistics, and corridor-based robotic operations. In many practical settings, agents are constrained to predefined routes due to infrastructure or operational requirements. Under such conditions, collision avoidance cannot rely on spatial rerouting and must instead be achieved by coordinating the timing of motion along the routes. Existing approaches to multi-agent collision avoidance can be broadly categorized into reactive local methods and planning-based global methods. Reactive schemes, including reciprocal collision avoidance, safety-critical control, and model predictive control, provide fast responses with low computational overhead [1]–[8]. However, their reliance on local information often leads to conservatism or deadlocks in dense environments. Global coordination methods, such as priority-based planning, conflict-based search, sequen-tial convex programming, and mixed-integer formulations, leverage global information to produce more consistent solutions [9]–[14]. Nevertheless, these approaches primarily resolve conflicts through spatial path modification, which is incompatible with route-constrained systems.

Temporal scheduling offers a natural alternative by ad-justing agent velocities while preserving fixed paths. A representative framework determines passing orders at conflict regions via mixed-integer programming and subsequently generates dynamically feasible trajectories through convex optimization [15]. While effective for moderate problem sizes, such hierarchical methods suffer from poor scalability due to the combinatorial growth of discrete sequencing variables, and may exhibit numerical sensitivity in dense scenarios. To alleviate these limitations, recent approaches attempt to avoid explicit sequencing. For instance, [16] formulates colli-sion avoidance through projection onto nonconvex constraints at intersection points. Although this reduces combinatorial complexity, its event-based formulation is limited in handling shallow-angle crossings or prolonged proximity between agents. Motivated by these challenges, this paper proposes a distance-based velocity scheduling framework for multi-agent systems on predefined routes. Waypoint passage times are treated as optimization variables, and a differentiable surrogate trajectory model maps these timings to continuous position profiles while capturing first-order tracking dynamics. Based on this representation, pairwise safety is enforced through distance-based penalties evaluated over the mission horizon. The resulting nonlinear and nonconvex problem is solved using an inexact-projection Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) framework that combines structured timing updates with gradient-based collision correction, thereby avoiding explicit integer sequencing variables. The main contributions of this paper are threefold. First, we formulate route-constrained multi-agent coordination as a waypoint-time optimization problem with distance-based safety constraints defined over the mission horizon. Second, we develop a differentiable surrogate trajectory model and an inexact-projection ADMM solver tailored to the resulting nonconvex structure. Third, we validate the proposed approach in random-crossing, bottleneck, and graph-based network scenarios, and compare it with a representative hierarchical temporal-scheduling baseline [15]. The results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves feasible and time-efficient coordination across a range of congestion conditions without requiring explicit priority assignment. II. B. Random Crossing 

To evaluate the proposed algorithm in congested multi-agent environments, we consider random crossing scenarios in which agents traverse a shared central region while following predefined waypoint paths. Two configurations are examined. In the co-directional case, agents move in similar directions and conflicts arise from overtaking interactions. In the counter-directional case, agents approach from opposite directions, producing head-on conflicts. Because the proposed method resolves conflicts without spatial path deviation, head-on encounters are gener-ally more challenging than overtaking interactions and may become infeasible under certain conditions [15]. Therefore, the counter-directional scenario is further evaluated through Monte Carlo simulations. 

a) Overtaking (Co-Directional Random Crossing) 

The co-directional random crossing scenario represents a relatively tractable case in which agents move in similar directions and conflicts occur primarily through overtaking interactions. Fig. 3 shows the waypoint trajectories for this scenario. Agents start from distributed initial positions and traverse a congested central region toward randomly perturbed goals, generating multiple potential overtaking conflicts. The resulting velocity profiles and inter-agent distances are shown in Fig. 4. The velocity trajectories exhibit smooth adjustments that resolve conflicts without altering the spatial paths. The distance trajectories confirm that all pairwise separations remain above the safety threshold dsafe = 1 mthroughout the mission. For all successful trials, however, the algorithm consistently converges to feasible solutions with relatively small travel-time overhead.

The key technical ingredient is a differentiable surrogate trajectory model that maps waypoint timings to smooth posi-tion profiles and enables distance-based safety penalties to be evaluated over the mission horizon. The resulting nonconvex problem was solved using an inexact-projection ADMM algo-rithm that combines structured timing updates with gradient-based collision-correction steps while avoiding explicit integer sequencing variables. Numerical results on random-crossing, bottleneck, and graph-based network scenarios showed that the proposed method computes feasible and time-efficient schedules across a range of congestion conditions. In the tested bottleneck cases, it also achieved shorter mission completion times than a representative hierarchical MIP– SOCP baseline. Future work will focus on strengthening the theoretical characterization of the surrogate model and temporal dis-cretization, improving robustness under higher-fidelity vehicle dynamics, and extending the framework to larger networked systems with uncertainty and online replanning.
