## On the Limits of Prediction: Forecastability Profiles and Information Decay in Time Series 

Peter Maurice Catt Independent Researcher Auckland, New Zealand 

Abstract —Forecasting accuracy is bounded by the information available about the future. This paper makes that statement precise using information-theoretic tools. Under logarithmic loss, the ex-pected performance of any probabilistic forecast decomposes into two parts: an irreducible component and an approximation component. The irreducible term is the conditional entropy of the future given the available information, while the approximation term is the divergence between the true conditional distribution and the forecasting method. The gap between this conditional-entropy limit and an unconditional baseline is exactly the mutual information between the future obser-vation and the declared information set. This leads to a definition of forecastability as the maximum achiev-able reduction in expected log loss. Evaluated across horizons, fore-castability forms a profile that describes how predictive information varies with lead time. This profile reflects the dependence structure of the process and need not be monotone: predictive information may be concentrated at particular lags, including seasonal horizons, even when intermediate horizons contain little useful signal. From this profile, the paper defines the informative horizon set: the horizons at which forecastability exceeds a practical threshold. At horizons not in this set, the achievable gain over the unconditional baseline is necessarily small, regardless of the forecasting method used. The framework therefore separates what is learnable from what is not, and distinguishes limits imposed by the data from errors introduced by modelling. The result is a pre-modelling diagnostic that identifies where meaningful prediction is feasible before any model is chosen, providing a principled basis for allocating modelling effort across forecast horizons. 

Index Terms —Forecastability profile, informative horizon set, mutual information, conditional entropy, log loss, prediction limits, proper scoring rule, information decay. 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Forecasting is usually framed as a problem of model choice. This paper starts earlier. It asks how much predictive informa-tion the available data contain at each horizon, and what that implies for the best accuracy any method can achieve. The central object is the forecastability profile : the function 

F(h; It) that maps each forecast horizon to the mutual infor-mation between the future observation and the declared infor-mation set. This profile is horizon-dependent and reflects the dependence structure of the process. The primary operational output is the informative horizon set Hε: the collection of horizons at which forecastability exceeds a decision-relevant threshold ε. At horizons outside Hε, the maximum achievable reduction in expected log loss relative to the unconditional baseline is bounded by ε, given the declared information set and under logarithmic loss. Forecastability is not a single threshold crossing but a struc-ture over horizons. Decisions about where to invest modelling effort should be based on the set of horizons where this structure exceeds a decision-relevant threshold. The paper makes three contributions. First, it shows that the maximum achievable reduction in expected log loss from exploiting any information set is exactly the mutual infor-mation between the future observation and that information set (Theorem 2). This quantity, termed forecastability, defines a horizon-dependent upper bound on predictive improvement under logarithmic loss and relative to the unconditional base-line. Second, it introduces the forecastability profile and the in-formative horizon set as the primary diagnostic and operational objects, and shows that at any horizon where F(h; It) ≤ ε, the gain from any probabilistic forecasting procedure is bounded by ε (Proposition 4). Third, it links one-step forecastability to the entropy rate of the process (Proposition 3), anchoring the profile in classical information theory. The underlying identities are standard. The contribution is to show that they imply hard, horizon-dependent forecasting limits and to formalise the forecastability profile and infor-mative horizon set as the objects through which these limits become operational for practice. The primary object of anal-ysis is the forecastability profile F(h; It). Decision-relevant quantities are derived from this profile, in particular the set of horizons for which forecastability exceeds a threshold. This reframes poor forecasting performance not primarily as model inadequacy, but as insufficient predictive information in the declared information set relative to the horizon of interest. The paper draws on Shannon [31], Kullback [23], Fano [13], R´ enyi [29], Cover and Thomas [8], Gray [17], Csisz´ ar and K¨ orner [9], the theory of proper scoring rules [14], and the concept of forecastability as an information-theoretic di-agnostic [4]–[6]. It is also related to information-theoretic pre-dictability work in geophysical science [10], [11], [21], [24]. The present paper differs in focus: rather than characterising predictability within a given dynamical model, it establishes model-independent limits on forecasting performance under logarithmic loss and introduces the forecastability profile and informative horizon set as decision tools for practice. 

A. Relation to Existing Forecasting Practice 

Standard forecasting workflows typically proceed from model selection to evaluation, with performance assessed rela-tive to competing methods. While this approach is effective for comparing models, it does not address whether the data itself contains sufficient information to support accurate prediction at the horizon of interest. As a result, poor performance relative to the unconditional baseline is often attributed to model choice rather than to limitations in the underlying information structure. 1

The framework developed in this paper complements ex-isting practice by introducing a prior question: how much of the future is predictable from the available information? By quantifying forecastability as mutual information and by identifying the horizons at which this information exceeds or falls below a decision-relevant threshold, the analysis shifts the focus from model comparison to feasibility assessment. This provides a theoretical basis for determining when further modelling effort is warranted and when it is not. We formalise this through the forecastability profile and the informative horizon set, decision-theoretic constructs derived from information-theoretic principles that identify the horizons at which prediction is informative and those at which it is not. The paper is organised as follows. Section II defines the setting and states the regularity conditions. Section III es-tablishes the information bound on prediction. Section IV introduces the forecastability profile and informative horizon set. Section V gives the loss decomposition. Section VI charac-terises behaviour at horizons of low forecastability. A. Forecastability Definition 1 (Forecastability): The forecastability of the process {Yt} at horizon h ≥ 1 relative to information set It

is 

F(h; It) = I(Yt+h; It). (6) Forecastability is mutual information under a declared in-formation set. By Theorem 2, it is the maximum number of nats by which any probabilistic method can improve on the marginal predictor at horizon h, under logarithmic loss. Forecastability should be interpreted as a horizon-resolved profile rather than a scalar quantity. The shape of this profile reflects the temporal structure of dependence in the data. It is horizon-dependent and may be non-monotone: in many practical series, forecastability is concentrated at specific lags, particularly seasonal periods, rather than distributed smoothly across horizons. 

B. Behaviour of Forecastability Across Horizons 

Forecastability varies across horizons and is determined by the dependence between the future and the declared informa-tion set. At horizons where this dependence is weak, the avail-able predictive information is limited, regardless of whether those horizons are short or long. Under mixing conditions with sufficient regularity on the joint distribution, the mutual information I(Yt+h; It) converges to zero as h → ∞ [17]. However, convergence to zero should not be assumed to hold automatically for arbitrary processes on general state spaces; it requires that the mixing rate is strong enough to control the mutual information. The informative horizon set, defined below, captures the horizons at which predictive information remains decision-relevant.

D. The Informative Horizon Set 

Given the forecastability profile and a decision-relevant threshold ε > 0, the central operational object is the set of horizons at which modelling effort may yield meaningful gains. 

Definition 2 (Informative horizon set): For a chosen thresh-old ε > 0, the informative horizon set is 

Hε = {h ≥ 1 : F(h; It) > ε } . (8) The set Hε provides the operational output of the frame-work. It defines the horizons at which predictive information exceeds the decision-relevant threshold. This set, rather than any single crossing point, determines where modelling effort is justified. In practice, decisions should be based on Hε, not on any single threshold crossing. The maximum informative horizon is hmax (ε) = sup Hε, providing the most distant horizon at which predictive information remains actionable. For non-monotone forecastability profiles, Hε may be a non-contiguous set: a seasonal series might have horizons 1–7 and 11–13 in Hε, with horizons 8–10 outside it. The threshold ε is a decision parameter: the minimum im-provement in expected log loss that justifies modelling effort. It is not an intrinsic property of the process. In practice, ε may be chosen with reference to the cost of model development and maintenance, the tolerated degradation in forecast quality, the decision value at stake, or the cost of forecast error in the application domain. This paper does not prescribe a specific method for selecting ε; the framework is valid for any positive threshold. 

E. Interpretation of the Forecastability Profile 

The forecastability profile partitions the horizon space into regions where modelling effort has different expected returns. At horizons in Hε, predictive information remains sufficient for probabilistic models to meaningfully reduce expected log loss relative to the unconditional baseline. In this regime, improvements in model specification, feature engineering, or estimation can translate into measurable gains in performance. At horizons outside Hε, the remaining mutual information between the information set and the future outcome is small. In this regime, the expected reduction in log loss achievable by any forecasting method is bounded by ε relative to the unconditional baseline. Consequently, improvements in model complexity yield diminishing returns. This interpretation reframes poor forecasting performance. Rather than necessarily indicating model inadequacy, weak performance relative to the unconditional baseline at certain horizons may reflect the absence of exploitable structure in the declared information set at those horizons. Conversely, high absolute loss at a horizon within Hε may still be near-optimal if the irreducible component is large. The profile therefore provides a diagnostic that separates model-limited regimes from information-limited regimes. 3F. At horizons where forecastability is small, the irreducible floor is high. When F(h; It) ≤ ε,

H(Yt+h | I t) ≥ H(Yt+h) − ε. (14) At such horizons, the best possible probabilistic forecast is already close to the unconditional baseline in expected log loss: the maximum achievable improvement is at most ε. Low forecastability does not imply that prediction is impossible, only that the maximum achievable improvement over the unconditional baseline is small under logarithmic loss. When this condition holds across a range of horizons, modelling effort yields limited returns throughout that range. Under mixing conditions with sufficient regularity, fore-castability converges to zero as h → ∞ [17], but the profile may take many shapes along the way. The rate and shape of the forecastability profile are process- and frequency-dependent [6], [26]. The informative horizon set Hε summarises which horizons retain sufficient predictive information to justify modelling effort. VII. E MPIRICAL ESTIMATION 

The results above are stated in terms of the true mu-tual information. In practice, I(Yt+h; It) must be estimated from data using non-parametric estimators such as k-nearest-neighbour, kernel-based, or partitioning methods [20], [27]. Estimation quality depends on sample size, dimensionality, and dependence strength. In low-dimensional settings, such as lag-based information sets with a small number of em-bedding dimensions, k-nearest-neighbour estimators provide reliable estimates of the forecastability profile [6], [20]. In higher-dimensional settings, such as rich covariate histories or multivariate information sets, estimation becomes substantially more difficult: finite-sample bias increases, variance grows, and the computational cost of the diagnostic may approach that of fitting a forecasting model. The framework is conceptually prior to modelling, in that it addresses feasibility before model selection, but it is not necessarily computationally cheaper than modelling in every setting. For short series or high-dimensional information sets, the overall shape of the estimated forecastability profile is often 4more reliable than any single threshold crossing. In practice, the informative horizon set is approximated by identifying the horizons at which the estimated mutual information exceeds ε

[6]. VIII. I MPLICATIONS FOR FORECASTING PRACTICE 

The central implication is that, under logarithmic loss and for the declared information set, forecasting is constrained by information before it is constrained by modelling. The forecastability profile acts as a pre-modelling diagnostic, iden-tifying where predictive structure exists before any model is specified. The informative horizon set Hε identifies the horizons at which predictive information exceeds a decision-relevant threshold and modelling effort may be justified. At horizons outside this set, the maximum return to additional model complexity is bounded by ε relative to the uncondi-tional baseline, and effort should instead be directed toward modifying the information set or the forecasting horizon. The decomposition in Section V also changes model eval-uation. Relative comparisons between methods remain use-ful, but they do not reveal how close a method is to the information-theoretic limit. A method can have high absolute loss and still be near-optimal if the irreducible component is large; conversely, poor performance relative to the uncon-ditional baseline at a horizon well within Hε indicates that the declared information set contains predictive information that the method is not exploiting, and therefore that model improvement may be productive. This perspective also clarifies the role of external variables. Better forecasts may come either from better models or from richer information sets, but these are fundamentally different interventions. The forecastability profile indicates which of the two is more likely to pay off at each horizon. The optimality of the conditional distribution under log loss, the role of mutual information, and the data process-ing inequality are classical results in information theory [8], [9], [17]. The contribution here is not a new information-theoretic identity, but the formal interpretation of these results as horizon-specific limits on achievable predictive improve-ment under logarithmic loss. Standard forecasting frameworks address model choice and combination [1], [2], [7], [30], [32]; the broader forecasting literature [3], [16], [19], [28] is oriented towards method development and evaluation [12], [18] rather than the prior question of whether the data support useful prediction at the target horizon. These frameworks do not typically formalise at which horizons modelling effort is no longer justified. In practice, model development is often driven by the expec-tation that additional complexity will yield improved accuracy. The information-theoretic framework presented in this paper imposes a strict limit on this expectation under logarithmic loss. When forecastability is high, improvements in model specification can reduce the divergence between the model and the true conditional distribution, thereby approaching the entropy-defined lower bound on loss. When forecastability is low, the achievable improvement in expected loss is tightly bounded. In such cases, increasing model complexity does not increase the available predictive information and can lead to overfitting, instability, and unnecessary computational cost without meaningful improvement in accuracy. The appropriate response at horizons of low forecastability is therefore not to refine the model, but to reconsider the problem formulation. This may involve shortening the forecast horizon, augmenting the information set, or reframing the decision problem. The forecastability profile and informative horizon set provide a principled basis for making this distinction. 

A. Illustrative Example: Seasonal Information Structure 

To illustrate how the forecastability profile changes a struc-tural decision that a single diagnostic would miss, consider a monthly series with strong annual seasonality. Suppose that the forecastability profile, estimated from data, takes the following schematic form: F(h) > ε for h ∈ { 1, 2, 3, 4, 11 , 12 , 13 }

and F(h) ≤ ε for h ∈ { 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10 }. The informative horizon set is Hε = {1, 2, 3, 4, 11 , 12 , 13 }: the short-range and seasonal-range horizons contain exploitable predictive information, while the intermediate horizons do not. A framework based on a single first-crossing diagnostic would report h = 5 as the point at which prediction becomes uninformative and would recommend against modelling effort at any longer horizon. The informative horizon set reveals that this conclusion is wrong: horizons 11–13 contain substantial predictive information because the seasonal dependence re-emerges at the annual lag. Now consider a change to the information set. Suppose an external leading indicator is added that is correlated with the target at horizons 6–8 but uncorrelated elsewhere. The updated profile may shift to F(h) > ε for h ∈{1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 7, 8, 11 , 12 , 13 }, expanding Hε from seven to ten horizons. This demonstrates that the informative horizon set is not a fixed property of the target series but changes structurally when the declared information set is enriched. The profile makes this change visible and actionable, whereas a comparison of competing models at a fixed horizon would not. IX. C ONCLUSION 

This paper has shown that, under logarithmic loss, fore-casting performance is bounded by the mutual information between the future and the declared information set. By formalising forecastability as this mutual information and introducing the forecastability profile and informative horizon set, we provide a framework for identifying the horizons at which prediction is informative and those at which it is not. The key implication is that forecasting is not solely a problem of model selection. For a given information set and under logarithmic loss, it is constrained by the structure of the data itself. When predictive information is present, models can approach the entropy-defined limits of performance. When it is absent, no increase in model complexity can compensate. This perspective reframes forecasting as a problem of in-formation before modelling. It suggests that the first question in any forecasting task should not be which model to use, but whether the declared information set contains sufficient predictive information at the horizon of interest. 5REFERENCES [1] H.
