Abstract 

We introduce the Geometric Evolution Graph Convolutional Network (GEGCN), a novel framework that enhances graph representation learning by modeling geometric evolution on graphs. Specifically, GEGCN employs a Long Short-Term Memory to model the structural sequence generated by discrete Ricci flow, and the learned dynamic representations are infused into a Graph Convolutional Network. Extensive experiments demonstrate that GEGCN achieves state-of-the-art performance on classification tasks across various benchmark datasets, with its performance being particularly outstanding on heterophilic graphs. 

Keywords: Ricci Flow, Graph Convolutional Network, LSTM, Graph Representation Learning. 

# 1 Introduction 

Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), especially Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) and their variants, have achieved remarkable success for learning representations on graph-structured data, by iteratively propagating and aggregating information from a node’s local neighborhood [1]. Despite their effectiveness, most existing GNNs, such as GCN [2], GAT [3], and GraphSAGE [4], treat graphs as static snapshots, and focus on local message passing. This paradigm often overlooks the rich, global geometric properties inherent in graph structures. Recently, the concept of discrete curvature [5– 7] has been successfully adapted to network anal-ysis. An expanding corpus of research confirms its utility as a principled way to characterize local and global structural features of graphs, such as bottlenecks [8], community structure [9, 10], and network fragility [11]. This geometric perspective has naturally inspired notable ef-forts to integrate discrete curvature into GNN pipelines [8, 12, 13]. A critical observation, how-ever, is that these methods rely on static, pre-computed curvature, thereby capturing only a single snapshot of graph geometry. Discrete Ricci flow [5, 14–16], which deforms a metric space by iteratively redistributing cur-vature, offers a natural mechanism for modeling graph evolution, promising a deep, multi-scale understanding of graph structure [10, 17, 18]. Despite the theoretical appeal and structural utility of Ricci flow, a natural question remains rarely explored: how to systematically integrate Ricci flow into GNNs to learn more powerful node representations. In parallel, recurrent neural networks (RNNs), 1

especially Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks [19], have demonstrated strong capa-bility in modeling temporal dependencies and structured sequences. Their effectiveness in cap-turing long-range dependence makes them suit-able for learning from sequential data generated by dynamic systems. This makes them a par-ticularly promising candidate for modeling the sequential graph structure generated by discrete Ricci flow, which is a natural dynamic process. Motivated by these observations, we propose the Geometric Evolution Graph Convolutional Network (GEGCN), a novel framework that in-tegrates Ricci flow with deep graph models by explicitly modeling geometric evolution as a se-quential process. Specifically, we generate a sequence of evolving graphs via discrete Ricci flow, where each step reveals structural informa-tion at a different scale, and employ an LSTM to capture the structural dynamics. The learned multi-scale structural information is then in-corporated into a GCN to enhance node em-beddings for downstream tasks. In this way, GEGCN bridges discrete differential geometry and graph neural networks through a dynamic representation learning paradigm. Extensive experimental results on multiple benchmark datasets demonstrate that GEGCN consistently achieves state-of-the-art perfor-mance for node classification. Further analysis shows that GEGCN better captures structural heterophily and long-range interactions than existing baselines, leading to more expressive graph representations. These results highlight the importance of modeling geometric dynamics for graph learning and suggest a promising direc-tion for unifying geometric analysis and graph neural networks. Our contributions are summarized as follows: 

(i) A New Geometric Perspective. We are among the first to introduce discrete Ricci flow into deep graph representation learning, providing a principled method to model the geometric evolution of graph structures. 

(ii) A Novel and Simple Architecture. 

GEGCN effectively integrates Ricci flow, LSTM, and GCN into a cohesive model for learning evolution-enhanced node representations. Built upon a mathematical framework of the geomet-ric evolution of graphs, our model achieves both strong performance and architectural simplicity. 

(iii) Empirical Superiority. Extensive ex-periments on multiple benchmarks show that GEGCN consistently outperforms strong base-lines, establishing excellent performance and underscoring the critical value of geometric evo-lution. 

# 2 Related Works 

Our work lies at the intersection of geometry of graphs, neural differential equations, and graph neural networks. We categorize related research into two primary strands, highlighting their dis-tinctions from our proposed GEGCN. 

## 2.1 Geometric Graph Neural Net-works 

A significant line of research enhances GNNs by incorporating discrete curvature or related metrics to modulate message passing. For in-stance, CurvGN [12] and CGNN [20] explic-itly use Ollivier-Ricci curvature to reweight message passing, demonstrating improved per-formance. κHGCN [21] utilizes a curvature-aware hyperbolic graph convolutional neural network in order to guide message passing of tree-like structures. SelfRGNN [22] tackles tem-poral graphs by formulating a time-dependent functional curvature to reweight message pass-ing. RC-UFG [23] introduces two curvature-based framelet models, namely RC-UFG (Hom) and RC-UFG (Het), showcasing their enhanced adaptability on homophilic and heterophilic graph datasets. In a different direction, Curv-Drop [13] introduces a curvature-based dropout sampling technique to mitigate oversmoothing 2and oversquashing. CurvGIB [24] advances this by applying a variational information bottle-neck principle, aiming to learn optimal informa-tion transport patterns for better node repre-sentations. It is worth noting that Topping et al. [8] formally established the connection be-tween oversquashing phenomena in GNNs and graph curvature. 

## 2.2 Neural Diffusion Equations on Graphs 

Another category frames deep learning on graphs as diffusion processes. GRAND [25] and Beltrami flow [26] model feature propaga-tion with diffusion equations, providing a prin-cipled, continuous-depth perspective on GNNs. Specifically, Beltrami flow employs the Laplace-Beltrami operator within a diffusion partial differential equation (PDE), making it a non-Euclidean diffusion process where node fea-tures are augmented with topology-derived posi-tional encodings. Closely related is GNRF [27], which conceptualizes node feature learning as a curvature-driven diffusion. In GNRF, curva-ture is predicted by an auxiliary neural module (EdgeNet) integrated within a diffusion PDE. Although nominally linked to Ricci flow, GNRF essentially implements a learned, curvature-informed diffusion PDE rather than an explicit discrete Ricci flow that evolves the graph struc-ture itself to generate a geometric sequence. 

## 2.3 Summary and Positioning 

Collectively, while geometric GNNs validate the utility of curvature, they predominantly rely on a static curvature snapshot, whether pre-computed or learned as a fixed parameter. This captures only a single-scale geometric view and fails to model the intrinsic multi-scale evolution of graph structure. In contrast, our model em-ploys discrete Ricci flow as a dynamic sequence generator to produce a multi-scale geometric evolution of the graph. Similarly, while PDE-based diffusion models are powerful, they typically do not incorporate the geometric evolution enabled by discretized Ricci flow, thereby missing the opportunity to capture the inherent multi-scale geometric in-formation in graph structures. Our GEGCN evolves the graph geometry via discrete Ricci flow and use an LSTM to learn from the result-ing sequence of graphs, thereby capturing the full trajectory of geometric change.

## 3.3 Recurrent Neural Networks and Long Short-Term Mem-ory 

RNNs offer a natural framework for modeling sequential data, enabling them to process the dynamic graph sequences. However, standard RNNs suffer from the vanishing gradient prob-lem, where gradients diminish exponentially dur-ing backpropagation, severely limiting their abil-ity to learn long-range dependencies. LSTMs introduce a gated cell state that en-ables selective retention and forgetting of infor-mation across long sequences. The forget gate determines what information from the previous cell state should be retained or discarded. The input gate regulates the incorporation of new candidate information. The output gate con-trols which aspects of the updated cell state are propagated to the hidden state. This gating mechanism enables LSTMs to maintain stable gradients over long sequences and selectively preserve long-term dependencies while flexibly integrating new inputs. This capability makes LSTMs particularly 4suitable for modeling the geometric evolution se-quences produced by discrete Ricci flow. In our framework, the sequences of curvature and edge weights generated by the flow are fed into an LSTM encoder, which extracts latent represen-tations of the geometric dynamics. The encoded temporal-geometric features are then integrated into subsequent graph convolution layers. This design enables the model to capture multi-scale structural evolution, yielding richer and more expressive node representations than those ob-tained from standard GCNs operating on static graph snapshots. 

# 4 Methodology 

The proposed Geometric Evolution Graph Con-volutional Network is designed to unveil the multi-scale structural dynamics of graphs by in-tegrating discrete Ricci flow with sequential deep learning. GEGCN comprises three key compo-nents (Figure 1): (i) a Geometric Evolution Generator that produces a graph sequence via discrete Ricci flow; (ii) a Structural Dynamics Encoder based on LSTM; and (iii) a Curvature-aware Graph Convolution module for learning final node representations. 

## 4.1 Geometric Evolution Genera-tor via Discrete Ricci Flow 

This module generates a multi-scale geometric evolution of the input graph via discrete Ricci flow. Given an input graph G = ( V, E), each edge e ∈ E is assigned an initial weight we(0), which is typically set to 1. We construct a temporal graph sequence by the discrete Ricci flow. The process begins by computing the initial discrete Ricci curvature 

κe(0) for every edge e ∈ E. We then per-form T iterations of the discrete Ricci flow. In each iteration t, after updating the edge weight we(t) according to Eq. (3) , we recalcu-late the corresponding Ricci curvature κe(t) and the shortest-path distance ρe(t) based on the updated weighted graph. This iterative proce-dure produces, for each edge e, two evolution sequences that encode its geometric dynamics. One is the curvature sequence 

Ke = [ κe(0) , κ e(1) , . . . , κ e(T )] ,

and another is the weight sequence 

We = [ we(0) , w e(1) , . . . , w e(T )] .

Conceptually, while the classical Ricci flow on a smooth manifold defines a continuous geo-metric deformation that preserves fundamental structures, its discrete counterpart in Eq. (3) induces a stepwise evolution of the graph, pre-serving its underlying adjacency topology.

# 5 Experiments 

## 5.1 Experimental Setup 

Datasets. We evaluate the proposed GEGCN model on a comprehensive suite of benchmark datasets to assess its performance across varying graph topologies. For homophilic scenarios, we utilize three citation networks (Cora, Citeseer, and Pubmed [31]), the Coauthor CS network based on the Microsoft Academic Graph from the KDD Cup 2016 challenge, and the Amazon Photos product network [32]. Following the partitioning scheme in [23], we employ a uniform 20%/10%/70% split for training, validation, and testing. To further investigate the model’s robustness in heterophilic settings, we extend our evalua-tion to datasets where long-range dependencies are critical, including Cornell, Texas, and Wis-consin from WebKB [33], as well as Chameleon [34] and Actor [35]. For these heterophilic bench-marks, we adopt the experimental protocol from [8], using a 60%/20%/20% data split. 

Comparison Methods. In the evaluation on homophilic datasets, we compare our model against a diverse set of competitive baselines ranging from classical GNNs to spectral-based methods. These include MLP, GCN [2], GAT [3], and GraphSAGE [4]. We also include models designed for deeper or more flexible propagation, such as JKNet [36], APPNP [37], GPRGNN [38], MoNet [39], and UFGConv [40]. Moreover, we consider the geometry-based ap-proaches CurvGN [12] and RC-UFG [23]. For heterophilic benchmarks, the focus shifts to mitigating the oversquashing phenomenon, which [8] attributes to graph bottlenecks char-acterized by high negative curvature. To this end, we benchmark GEGCN against specialized architectures and graph-rewiring strategies: (i) GCN as a fundamental backbone; (ii) DIGL [41], which employs diffusion to improve graph con-nectivity; (iii) +FA [42], which introduces a fully adjacent layer to bypass structural bottlenecks; and (iv) SDRF [8], a surgical rewiring method based on Discrete Ricci Flow. We reproduce the experiments of SDRF 1 to ensure a fair compar-ison with our method. The average results of 10 random experiments are reported, and the top first, second, and third are highlighted. In our experiments, hyperparameters were optimized via random search to maximize the mean accuracy on the validation set.

Node Classification in Heterophilic Graph. 

Table 2 reports node classification perfor-mance on five widely used heterophilic bench-marks. Across all datasets, GEGCN consis-tently achieves the best accuracy. Compared with the strongest competing baselines, the per-formance improvement ranges from a moderate gain on Texas to substantial gains on Actor and Chameleon. For the latter, GEGCN’s accuracy represents a relative improvement of more than 30% over the strongest baselines (translating to absolute increases of 8 .76 and 16 .26 percent-age points, respectively). This pattern indicates that the advantage of GEGCN becomes more pronounced for heterophilic graphs. It is notable that the methods relying on global structural modifications, such as diffusion-based preprocessing and curvature-guided rewiring, exhibit dataset-dependent be-havior. While these approaches can partially al-leviate connectivity sparsity, their performance varies considerably across benchmarks. For in-stance, DIGL offers limited or inconsistent im-provements on Texas and Wisconsin (Table 2), despite enhancing graph connectivity. This sug-gests that, in heterophilic graphs, increasing connectivity alone is insufficient and may even be detrimental when newly introduced paths predominantly link nodes with dissimilar labels. In contrast, GEGCN embeds geometric infor-mation into the message-passing process rather than treating it as an external preprocessing step. This design enables curvature to mod-ulate the propagation of information between 8nodes, rather than altering the graph topology. As a result, GEGCN can selectively amplify task-relevant signals while suppressing mislead-ing aggregation effects that commonly arise in heterophilic settings. The advantage of this integrated approach is further reflected on Chameleon and Actor, where long-range dependencies play a critical role. Fully connected or aggressively densified graphs (+FA) fail to deliver comparable gains, despite theoretically eliminating distance-based bottle-necks. This contrast suggests that oversquash-ing in heterophilic graphs is not only caused by limited information propagation range, but also by indiscriminate aggregation of heterogeneous signals. By evolving the geometry of message passing in a curvature-aware manner, GEGCN provides a more principled mechanism for long-range information flow without sacrificing dis-criminative locality. Overall, these results suggest that for het-erophilic node classification, performance is lim-ited less by the absence of connections than by how information is propagated across neighbor-hoods where structural proximity does not align with semantic similarity. GEGCN mitigates this issue by embedding geometric evolution within the convolution process, resulting in sta-ble and consistent gains across a wide range of heterophilic datasets.

The computational complexity of the geomet-ric evolution process is primarily governed by the calculation of the 1-Wasserstein distance for each edge. While exact solvers for this lin-ear programming problem typically incur an 

O(|E|D3) complexity (where D is the average node degree), which can be intensive for large-scale graphs, this burden is effectively mitigated by employing the Sinkhorn Algorithm [43]. By introducing entropic regularization, the prob-lem is transformed into a matrix scaling task with near O(|E|D2) complexity. Furthermore, the discrete Ricci flow is implemented as a one-time pre-processing step, which is decoupled from the neural network training. Consequently, the online training phase only accounts for the LSTM encoder and GCN layers, both of which are linear with respect to the number of edges, ensuring the overall GEGCN framework remains efficient and feasible for practical applications. 

# 6 Conclusion 

The core idea of this paper is to model the evo-lutionary sequences generated by Ricci flow as a temporal process and integrate it seamlessly into a graph deep learning framework. Our framework consists of three stages: (i) We first apply discrete Ricci flow to the input graph to generate multi-scale geometric sequences. (ii) An LSTM encoder is then employed to pro-cess this sequence, learning to encode the tra-jectory of geometric changes into a compact representation. (iii) Finally, these geometric-aware representations are infused as enhanced features into a standard GCN, which performs the downstream prediction tasks. This design allows GEGCN to simultaneously leverage local neighborhood information (via GCN) and global, multi-scale geometric dynamics (via Ricci flow and LSTM). As a result, GEGCN demonstrates superior node representation learning capabil-ity. Extensive comparisons with various deep graph learning algorithms on a broad range of benchmark datasets show that it achieves state-of-the-art results in almost all cases (ranking first on most datasets and second on only one).

Therefore, in this work, we adopt this latter formulation as our discrete Ricci flow model for networks.
