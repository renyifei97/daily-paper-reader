# Thinking While Listening: Fast–Slow Recurrence for Long-Horizon Sequential Modeling 

Shota Takashiro 1 Masanori Koyama 1 Takeru Miyato 2 Yusuke Iwasawa 1 Yutaka Matsuo 1 Kohei Hayashi 1

## Abstract 

We extend the recent latent recurrent modeling to sequential input streams. By interleaving fast, recurrent latent updates with self-organizational ability between slow observation updates, our method facilitates the learning of stable internal structures that evolve alongside the input. This mechanism allows the model to maintain coherent and clustered representations over long horizons, improving out-of-distribution generalization in re-inforcement learning and algorithmic tasks com-pared to sequential baselines such as LSTM, state space models, and Transformer variants. 

## 1. Introduction 

Humans are capable of reasoning while simultaneously pro-cessing external inputs, allowing them to cope with long-horizon streams of variable lengths (Baddeley, 1992; Hasson et al., 2015). In such settings, an agent must maintain and update an internal state that summarizes past information while processing new observations, selectively extracting task-relevant signals and retaining them until future inputs resolve their relevance. Such a state-tracking type computa-tion is central to navigation, decision-making, and predic-tion in nonstationary environments (Kaelbling et al., 1998; Thrun, 2000; Hausknecht & Stone, 2015). However, learn-ing to reliably maintain such an internal state over long horizons is a well-known challenge for recurrent neural networks (Bengio et al., 1994; Pascanu et al., 2013) and it remains a major bottleneck in modern neural sequential models such as Transformers (Tay et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023; Sinha et al., 2025). To explore this endeavor, we draw on recent advances in latent recurrent modeling (Geiping et al., 2025; Hao et al., 2024a; Gladstone et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025; Miyato

## 2. Preliminary: Latent Recurrent Model 

Our method is inspired by models that conduct reasoning in the latent space by iterating a recurrent block (Gladstone et al., 2025; Geiping et al., 2025; Miyato et al., 2025). Given a specified number of recurrent iterations T at training time, this class of approaches takes an observation O as input together with an initial noise tensor X0 ∈ RK×D , where K

is the number of latent tokens and D is the model dimension. The model then iteratively updates the latent state X(t) by initializing X(0) = X0 and applying 

X(t + 1) = R (X(t), Encoder( O)) (1) for t = 0 , 1, . . . , T − 1. This produces X(T ), a latent con-clusion of depth T . We use Decoder( X(T )) for inference. This system can be applied autoregressively; for example, one may set Decoder( X(T )) to serve as the next observa-tion O in a subsequent round of reasoning. When applied autoregressively at turn index s, models in this family typi-cally use the accumulated set of the latent tokens Xs(T ), or its decoded form, as contextual input, and reinitialize Xs(0) 

at every turn (Gladstone et al., 2025; Hao et al., 2024a). A naive extension of this strategy to an evolving observa-tion sequence Os is to concatenate {Xs(T )}s with {Os}s

as context, which causes the context length to grow with increasing s. This introduces one source of long-horizon failure, namely the difficulty associated with the use of excessively long contexts (Liu et al., 2023; Shojaee et al., 2025). In the next section, we extend the latent recurrent model more directly so that the reasoning proceeds continu-ously while listening to an evolving stream of observations. 

## 3. Fast–Slow Coupling of Latent Recurrence with the Observational Process 

Our goal is to explore a system that can continuously inte-grate the evolving external signals into the latent reasoning process. To this end, we modify latent recurrent reasoning by considering a coupled dynamical system in which the observational process and the reasoning process evolve on the same timeline but at different speeds. In our model, the reasoning process proceeds T times faster than the observa-tion process, so that T also represents the number of latent reasoning iterations performed per observation. ## 4. Related Work 

Recent studies have given birth to many models that utilize recurrent mechanisms in the latent space. Unlike approaches based on chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022), Geiping et al. (2025) argue that such latent recur-rent approaches do not require specialized training data, can work with small context windows, and can capture types of reasoning that are not easily represented in the output space, such as words and symbols. Geiping et al. (2025), similar to Miyato et al. (2025), propose the framework of applying a recurrent module to a randomly initialized la-tent signal while injecting the conditional input. Du et al. (2022) formulates a relevant mechanism through energy minimization in EBMs, and Gladstone et al. (2025) pro-vides transformer-based instantiations. Unlike our approach, when we apply these approaches to sequential inputs, these methods decode each output, and the conditional context grows in length with the sequence. The same applies to Zhu et al. (2025) and Fan et al. (2025) as well. Coconut (Hao et al., 2024b), on the other hand, does not decode the output and conducts all computation of the sequence in the latent space. However, this approach also makes the conditional context grow in the latent space. Our approach carries over the latent variable over sequences of any length. Contexts are compressed and organized into a latent variable of fixed size through recurrence. As a recurrent method that conducts all computations in the latent space of fixed dimension, we shall mention Per-ceiver (Jaegle et al., 2021), which proposes an RNN-style latent update to incorporate sequential signals. While re-lated to our approach, this approach is not equipped with a recurrent reasoning loop that proceeds faster than the ob-servations. Adaptive Computation Time (Graves, 2017) is also closely related to our approach, as it separates the fast-loop RNN from the slow input dynamics. However, it does not apply a recurrent mechanism with a self-organization property like a transformer (Geshkovski et al., 2024), and the latent at the end of the fast loop is not directly carried over to the next loop. Instead, the initial latent of the next loop is produced by the ensemble of the trajectories in the previous fast loop. Universal Transformer (Dehghani et al., 2019) uses recurrent self-attention, but without input injec-tion. (Sch ¨one et al., 2025) is more related to our approach, except that they use SSM to model the fast process, and introduce another auxiliary variable in the fast process that 3Thinking While Listening 

is initialized at every loop. We shall also mention HRM (Wang et al., 2025), which is inspired by biological findings (Murray et al., 2014; Hunten-burg et al., 2018; Zeraati et al., 2023), as well as its exten-sion, TRM (Jolicoeur-Martineau, 2025). These methods also introduce a system of coupled fast and slow dynamics. However, unlike our approach, these methods do not con-sider temporally varying input sequences, and their reason-ing is conducted on a stationary input. Also, CTM (Darlow et al., 2025) applies fast recurrence in autoregressive time segments, and is capable of handling the temporally varying input sequences. CTM frames their architecture as a dy-namical system of interacting neurons (latents). Unlike our approach, they explicitly define the synchronization strength between each pair of neurons that is integrated over time, and produce the output from the synchronization pattern itself as opposed to our direct readout from the latents. 

## 5. Experiments 

We conducted a series of experiments to assess our model’s ability to dynamically integrate sequential inputs into its re-current process in support of correct inference. We selected tasks that fundamentally require the model to iteratively and selectively identify task-relevant information from an evolv-ing input sequence, and to consolidate it by continuously integrating this information into the latent state. We first present results on the egocentric maze and Dyck tasks, both of which provide supervision in the form of cor-rect answers. In these settings, the model can retrospectively infer which parts of the input are irrelevant to the correct underlying structure of the data. We additionally consider more challenging reinforcement learning tasks on MiniGrid. In these online settings, the model cannot directly compute the correct regret or value signal from immediate rewards, and therefore must learn indirectly how to integrate episodic information as it explores the environment.

5.1. Egocentric Maze 

The goal of this task is to infer the shortest path from the start to the end, while receiving a stream of egocentric ob-servations from a navigator exploring the maze. In this task, the information corresponding to dead-end paths is naturally task-irrelevant. In order for the model to be able to general-ize its inference capability to OOD mazes of large size, the model must learn to efficiently integrate the task-relevant information and iteratively consolidate its hypothesis about the shortest path. More specifically, the model in this task is given a sequence of local, cropped 7×7 neighborhood images Os correspond-ing to the egocentric observations of a navigator agent that explores the maze following the right-hand rule (the model has no control over the navigator). The navigator streams egocentric observations until the goal is reached. The de-coded output of the model is a sequence of movements ( up, down, left, right, pause ) corresponding to the estimated shortest path. Depending on the geometry of the maze, the length of the observation sequence S naturally varies across samples. As a supervisory signal to be com-pared against the decoded output, we provide the model, at the end of each episode, with the true shortest path for the maze, represented in the same format. We evaluate accuracy using a binary exact-match criterion on the entire predicted action sequence, after removing all pause tokens from both the prediction and the ground-truth shortest path.

Emergent structure in layerwise energies and latents 

Interestingly, through learning the prediction task, our model discovers latent reasoning that aligns with the struc-tural organization of the Dyck language. Recall that for the recurrence used in our experiments (Section 3.1), there exists an energy-like scalar that reflects the organizational level of the state. We show the evolution of this energy in Figure 7, and the evolution of the latent in Figure 8. The energy of X(1) spikes upon the arrival of a new token and then stabilizes at a level that depends on the bracket type and whether the current token is opening or closing. In contrast, the energy of X(2) is largely insensitive to bracket identity and is instead organized by stack depth . This qualitative shift in energy organization indicates that the second fast process abstracts away from token identity and operates at a higher structural level. PCA projections of the latents for both X(1) and X(2) support this interpretation: the state trajectories form a crisp, spring-shaped manifold indexed by stack depth that remains coherent over long horizons. This structured organization in the learned latent space is similar to the report in (Zhu et al., 2025) that the looped model tends to produce interpretable reasoning traces. This behavior stands in stark contrast to Mamba (Figure 8c). 

5.3. Reinforcement Learning 

We next evaluate our model on challenging partially observ-able reinforcement learning tasks from the MiniGrid envi-ronment (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2023). We consider three MiniGrid tasks: DoorKey, MultiRoom, and LavaCrossing (see Figure 15). In these navigation tasks, DoorKey requires the agent to pick up a key, open a door, and then reach the goal in this order. MultiRoom requires the agent to open multiple doors sequentially to reach the goal. LavaCross-ing requires the agent to reach the goal while avoiding the lava. In all tasks, the reward is provided only at the end of a successful episode (i.e., sparse rewards). Most importantly, because these tasks do not provide a ground-truth supervisory sequence of actions, it is more dif-ficult for the model to retrospectively infer which informa-tion in the episode is worth incorporating into its reasoning and consolidating into intermediate conclusions. At each slow step, the agent receives an egocentric 7 ×

7 observation with object/color/door-state channels. The agent then selects an action from the set as ∈ { left ,

right , forward , toggle , pickup , drop , done }.

Setup We designed our evaluation protocol to test the model’s generalization capabilities in a zero-shot transfer setting. The agent is trained exclusively on the simplest ID configuration and is subsequently evaluated, without any further training, on more complex and previously un-seen OOD environments, which are typically larger than the ID environments (see Table 4 in the Appendix for de-tails). ## 6. Conclusion 

We proposed a recurrent model that integrates sequential ob-servations into recurrent modeling. Across the tasks that re-quire long horizon information of task-relevant information while tending to incoming observation of varying relevance, our approach consistently outperformed strong baselines, including an LSTM, Mamba-2, and Transformer-XL, and it demonstrated competitive generalization and decision-making in dynamic environments. This suggests the cou-pling of fast recurrent latent dynamics with slowly evolving observation dynamics as a promising mechanism for sequen-tial inference over long-horizon. 

Limitations and future work Despite strong perfor-mance on controlled benchmarks, our study has several limitations. First, experiments are limited to relatively small-scale synthetic and grid-based domains, and we have not yet validated the approach on larger real-world tasks such as language, video, or robotics. Second, the proposed fast-slow architecture incurs computational overhead due to its double-loop structure, resulting in lower GPU efficiency and slower inference than standard baselines (Appendix, Figure 18); improving efficiency via recursion-friendly parallelization methods (e.g., (Danieli et al., 2025)) is an important future direction. Finally, we do note that there is a sensitivity to the random initialization, as observed in Figure 9. This suggests gradient instability from repeated layer reuse, as observed in RNNs (Pascanu et al., 2013), and motivates further architectural and optimization improvements.

## A. Model Details 

A.1. Implementation of J

In our fast update rule (Eq. (3) ), the interaction term J determines how latent tokens mix with one another while incorporating the encoded observation signal. We build on the attention-based design originally introduced in Miyato et al. (2025). The original implementation uses only attention as its core. We extend their J to an attention + MLP block: we first compute an attention update over the latent tokens (with the observation stream integrated additively), and then apply a position-wise MLP to the resulting features. The MLP uses a ReLU activation. We found that this simple extension yields a performance improvement on the maze task in particular. We use this implementation of J for all tasks reported in the paper. For the attention module, we use GTA (Miyato et al., 2023) as positional embeddings. Pseudocode is provided in Figure 12.   

> class J(): def init (dim, oscillator dim): self.attn = SelfAttention(dim) self.mlp = MLP(dim) self.omega = Omega(dim, oscillator dim) def forward(x, c): y = self.attn(x + c) y = self.mlp(x + c + y) y = proj(y, x) # projection onto the tangent space y = y + self.omega(x) return y

C.4. Inference cost 

Unlike SSMs and LSTMs, our proposed model performs T forward passes for each observation. To quantify the resulting inference speed, we measured wall-clock time on the MiniGrid RL task. All models were implemented in PyTorch without using optimizations such as torch.compile , and timing was recorded on a single NVIDIA GH200 core. The results are shown in Figure 18. With the default choice T = 5 , our method is roughly three times slower than the Mamba and Transformer baselines. We note that this computational overhead is partly due to software constraints: competing models rely on highly optimized CUDA kernels and mature library implementations, whereas our current implementation lacks comparable low-level optimizations. We therefore expect that specialized kernels and recursion-friendly parallelization strategies could substantially reduce this gap without changing the model architecture. 

C.5. Parameter size vs OOD performance 

As summarized in Table 3, our proposed model is roughly an order of magnitude smaller in parameter count than common sequence models. This is because the recurrent core is highly parameter-efficient, and because our implementation does not yet benefit from the kernel-level optimizations available for architectures such as LSTMs or SSMs; scaling up the model size would therefore lead to comparatively higher computational cost despite the smaller parameter count. A natural concern is therefore whether the observed generalization gains could be attributed to reduced overfitting due to the smaller model size. We scale the channel width of our model to {64, 128, 256 } and train on the maze task using the same data and protocol. Unless otherwise noted, all training hyperparameters follow Section 5.1 (see Appendix B.1). For this ablation, we set the batch size to 128 and adopt a width-scaling learning rate lr = p64 /channels × 10 −3.

Results Table 5 reports OOD accuracy (mean ± std over 3 seeds). The 256 -channel variant attains the best OOD performance, suggesting that increasing capacity does not necessarily lead to overfitting in this setting.
