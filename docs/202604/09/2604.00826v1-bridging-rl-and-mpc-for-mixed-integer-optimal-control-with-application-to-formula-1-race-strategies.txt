# Bridging RL and MPC for mixed-integer optimal control with application to Formula 1 race strategies 

Joschua W¬® uthrich, Romir Damle, Giona Fieni, Melanie N. Zeilinger, Christopher H. Onder, Andrea Carron 

Abstract ‚ÄĒ We propose a hybrid reinforcement learning (RL) and model predictive control (MPC) framework for mixed-integer optimal control, where discrete variables enter the cost and dynamics but not the constraints. Existing hierarchical approaches use RL only for the discrete action space, leaving continuous optimization to MPC. Unlike these methods, we train the RL agent on the full hybrid action space, ensuring consistency with the cost of the underlying Markov decision process. During deployment, the RL actor is rolled out over the prediction horizon to parametrize an integer-free nonlinear MPC through the discrete action sequence and provide acontinuous warm-start. The learned critic serves as a terminal cost to capture long-term performance. We prove recursive feasibility, and validate the framework on a Formula 1 race strategy problem. The hybrid method achieves near-optimal performance relative to an offline mixed-integer nonlinear program benchmark, outperforming a standalone RL agent. Moreover, the hybrid scheme enables adaptation to unseen dis-turbances through modular MPC extensions at zero retraining cost. 

I. INTRODUCTION Many real world systems must simultaneously make dis-crete and continuous decisions. Examples of such systems appear in various engineering domains. Heating and cooling systems combine discrete operating mode selection, such as direct solar supply, thermal storage discharge, and natural ventilation, with continuous control of heat pump power [1]. Industrial process control handles discrete decisions such as valve switching, pump scheduling or reactor mode selec-tion with continuous regulation of flow rates, temperatures and pressures [2]. Decision-making problems such as race strategies in Formula 1 (F1) racing [3] aim to optimize the continuous energy management of the hybrid power unit in combination with discrete choices regarding the pit stop strategy. From a control point of view, such systems naturally give rise to mixed-integer optimal control problems (MIOCPs). Model predictive control (MPC) [4] and reinforcement learn-ing (RL) [5] are two distinct approaches for solving optimal control problems (OCPs) with complementary strengths. MPC provides safety and feasibility guarantees through con-strained optimization problems, while RL naturally handles long-horizon sequential decision making.

In this paper, we propose a hybrid RL-MPC framework that exploits the complementary strengths of both methods for mixed-integer optimal control. 

A. Related work 

Recently, many research efforts have been made to com-bine RL and MPC in a unified framework for general OCPs to exploit their complementary strengths. Within such frameworks, RL and MPC play distinct but complementary roles. RL commonly learns an approximation of the value function through data-driven exploration, capturing long-horizon behavior across the full state space, while MPC refines this approximation online through constrained op-timization, replanning at each time step from the current state [6], [7]. This global-local perspective has emerged as a unifying lens for understanding RL-MPC integration, where RL supplies long-term information and MPC refines decisions locally under constraints. Three main streams of possible RL-MPC synthesis approaches can be distinguished: value-augmented MPC, differentiable MPC, and hierarchical RL-MPC schemes [8]. Value-augmented MPC integrates a learned value function as a terminal cost in the MPC objective, allowing the reduction of the required planning horizon while improving long-term performance [9], [10], [11]. However, existing formulations do not exploit the structure of mixed-integer OCPs, and the computational cost of mixed-integer optimization remains. Differentiable MPC replaces neural network function approx-imators with constrained optimization layers within policy-gradient or actor-critic updates [12], [13]. This yields more interpretable policies with explicit constraint handling, as the control actions are directly obtained from a parametrized optimization problem rather than a black-box network. How-ever, constrained optimization problems must be solved during both offline training and online deployment, making this approach computationally demanding. MIOCPs can be formulated in this setting [14], but increase the computational burden even more during both training and deployment. Hierarchical RL-MPC schemes decompose the problem across two levels, with RL approximating a global policy and MPC serving as a local refinement module. Examples include guided policy search [15], the MPC-Net [16], and related frameworks in which an MPC generates trajectories used for policy learning [17]. However, these methods do not specifically address the hybrid action space structure of mixed-integer OCPs. Existing hierarchical schemes address the structure of hy-brid action spaces by training an RL agent only on the

discrete action space [18], [19]. Their RL policy maps the current state to a full discrete action trajectory over the prediction horizon in a single forward pass. This trajectory parametrizes the MPC, which optimizes only the continuous actions. This separation, however, introduces a fundamental inconsistency with the underlying hybrid Markov decision process (MDP). In a hybrid MDP, the Q-function maps a state-action pair (s, a ) to the expected cumulative future reward, where a is a single hybrid action. A Q-function approximation is consistent with the hybrid MDP‚Äôs cost structure if it respects this mapping [5]. In [18], [19], how-ever, Q-functions are learned as mappings from the current state and predicted action trajectory, rather than from a single state-action pair. This means that the learned value function does not correspond to the Q-function of the hybrid MDP, and as a consequence, the RL agent cannot be guaranteed to optimize the correct long-term objective. Furthermore, since the RL agent is trained on discrete decisions alone, the coordination between discrete and continuous actions is merely captured indirectly. Beyond these shared limitations, [18] decouples the Q-function across prediction horizon steps, using a separate Q-function at each step, to manage the combinatorial action space size, whereas [19] uses predicted rather than closed-loop states for value function learning. Both of these modifications further widen the mismatch between the learned Q-function and that of the underlying hybrid MDP. 

B. Contribution 

The contributions of this paper are threefold. First, we propose a hybrid RL-MPC framework that trains the RL agent on the full hybrid action space, ensuring consistency with the underlying hybrid MDP. We focus on systems where discrete inputs affect the cost and dynamics but not the feasibility of constraints. During deployment, the RL actor is rolled out over the MPC prediction horizon using the system model to parametrize and warm-start a continuous MPC. The RL critic serves as a terminal cost, capturing long-term performance beyond the planning horizon. This reduces the online problem to a continuous OCP, enabling efficient deployment. Second, we prove that under a structural assumption on the integer decisions, the proposed framework guarantees closed-loop constraint satisfaction. Third, we validate the framework on an F1 race strategy case study, evaluating performance and adaptability of the hybrid RL-MPC framework.

HYBRID RL-MPC FRAMEWORK The complementary strengths identified in Section II motivate a structured combination of RL and MPC for mixed-integer control. RL provides a global cost approxima-tion, while MPC offers model-based optimization capable of enforcing constraints and refining continuous actions locally. NUMERICAL EXAMPLE To validate the proposed hybrid RL-MPC framework, we apply it to an F1 race strategy problem, with the goal to determine the discrete pit stop decisions and the continuous energy allocation over a race. This setting is a natural fit for our framework. The problem involves tightly coupled dis-crete and continuous actions and a well-defined performance objective. Furthermore, Assumption 1 is satisfied, since for any pit stop decision, the continuous energy allocation can always be chosen to maintain feasibility, as the pit stop decisions affect only the performance objective but not the feasibility of the energy constraints. We adopt the problem setup of [3] as our benchmark, where both a MINLP and a standalone RL agent were developed and evaluated. We will show that our proposed hybrid RL-MPC method outperforms the standalone RL agent while achieving near-optimal performance relative to the MINLP solution.

The resulting energy management is subject to strict reg-ulatory bounds on battery capacity, fuel load, and en-ergy deployment [30], [31]. C. Architectural adaptability

We compare a nominal and amodified hybrid RL-MPC implementation, both operating in the same closed-loop environment with traffic present. CONCLUSION We proposed a hybrid RL-MPC framework for mixed-integer optimal control. By training the RL agent on the full hybrid action space, the resulting hybrid policy is optimized with respect to a cost function consistent with the true underlying hybrid MDP. During deployment, the RL actor is rolled out over the prediction horizon to parametrize a continuous NLP-MPC through the discrete action sequence. Additionally, it provides a warm-start for the continuous variables and the learned critic serves as a terminal cost. We proved recursive feasibility under a structural assumption on the discrete decisions, and validated the framework on an F1 race strategy problem, outperforming a standalone RL agent and achieving near-optimal performance relative to an offline MINLP benchmark. The hybrid architecture further enables adaptation to unseen disturbances, demonstrated through a traffic scenario, yielding a 5.19 s advantage without any retraining of the RL components. Two directions are of particular interest for future work. First, deriving formal suboptimality bounds similar to [11] would strengthen the theoretical foundation. Second, relaxing Assumption 1 to settings where discrete decisions affect feasibility could be addressed through safety filters [34] or control barrier functions [35], extending the framework to a broader class of hybrid action space systems.
